# Learning Things the XY

## Quick Start

**Learning Things the XY** is a two-axis framework for rapidly understanding anything new—products, people, trends, technologies, businesses. Plot the phenomenon on two dimensions, examine their intersection, and you'll understand not just *what* exists, but *why* it exists as it does.

**The two axes:**
- **X-Axis (Time/Depth):** How did this come into being? What is its complete evolutionary story from origin to present?
- **Y-Axis (Space/Breadth):** Where does it stand today relative to competitors and alternatives in its category?

**The power:** Where X meets Y, you discover the true causal forces.

---

## Web Search Mandate

**This framework is for NEW phenomena — always search before analyzing.** Your training data is stale. Do not rely on it for current competitive position, metrics, or recent events.

**X-Axis queries (history):**
- `[name] founded when who founder`
- `[name] history timeline major milestones`
- `[name] strategic pivot turning point`

**Y-Axis queries (current position):**
- `[name] market share competitors [current year]`
- `[name] vs [competitor] comparison`
- `[name] latest news [current year]`
- `[name] user reviews sentiment reddit`

**Confidence:** Mark claims ⭐⭐⭐ (multiple recent sources), ⭐⭐ (single authoritative), or ⭐ (unverified). Build analysis on ⭐⭐+ only.

## The Four-Step Process

### Step 1: Map the X-Axis (Historical Trajectory)

**First: Gather Current Information**

Since this framework is designed for understanding NEW phenomena, the first step is to gather up-to-date information via web search:

**Search Strategy for X-Axis Research:**
1. **Search for origin story:** `[phenomenon name] founded history when who`
2. **Search for major timeline:** `[phenomenon name] timeline major milestones launches`
3. **Search for key decisions:** `[phenomenon name] strategic pivot turning point`
4. **Search for critical events:** `[phenomenon name] crisis inflection point challenges`
5. **Search for recent evolution:** `[phenomenon name] [current year] current developments latest news`

**Then: Answer These Questions to Build the Complete Story**

Once you have current information from web searches, synthesize it:

1. **When was this created?** Who created it, and what was their motivation?
2. **What was the founding insight?** What problem or opportunity did they see?
3. **What were the major inflection points?** When did it pivot, accelerate, or face crisis? Why?
4. **What decisions accumulated advantage?** What early bets are paying off today?
5. **What path dependencies exist?** What past choices now constrain or enable current behavior?

**Output:** A coherent narrative timeline showing how today's state is the logical outcome of past decisions.

**Example question to ask yourself:** "If this decision hadn't been made in [year], would they be in this position today?"

**Web Search Best Practices for X-Axis:**
- Search for company/founder interviews (reveals original intent)
- Search for product release announcements (show strategic shifts)
- Search for historical articles and retrospectives
- Cross-reference multiple sources to verify timeline accuracy
- Look for "why we built this" founder statements

---

### Step 2: Map the Y-Axis (Competitive Position)

**First: Gather Current Information**

The Y-axis is about RIGHT NOW. Web search is essential because competitive landscapes shift weekly.

**Search Strategy for Y-Axis Research:**
1. **Current market position:** `[phenomenon name] market share [current year]`
2. **Direct competitors:** `[phenomenon name] competitors comparison alternatives`
3. **User/Customer reviews:** `[phenomenon name] vs [competitor] which is better`
4. **Recent news and analysis:** `[phenomenon name] [current year] news analysis opinion`
5. **Growth and momentum:** `[phenomenon name] user growth revenue metrics latest`
6. **Competitive positioning:** `[competitor] vs [phenomenon name] advantages disadvantages`
7. **Customer sentiment:** `[phenomenon name] reviews ratings user feedback [current year]`

**Then: Answer These Questions about Current Position**

Once you have current competitive data from web searches:

1. **Who competes in this space?** Direct competitors, indirect substitutes, emerging threats?
2. **How is this different from competitors?** On which dimensions does it win, lose, or tie?
3. **Why do users/customers choose this over alternatives?** What value are they optimizing for?
4. **What is its market position?** Is it dominant, niche, growing, declining?
5. **What are the structural vulnerabilities?** What could displace it?

**Output:** A competitive map showing this phenomenon's unique position and value proposition.

**Example question to ask yourself:** "If I had to explain to someone why they should pick this instead of competitor X, what's my best argument?"

**Web Search Best Practices for Y-Axis:**
- Search for recent product comparisons and reviews
- Look for market research reports with current data
- Check news outlets for recent competitive moves
- Review user/customer comments on Reddit, ProductHunt, Twitter/X
- Search for analyst reports and industry coverage
- Look for pricing and feature comparison articles
- Find recent competitor launches and their positioning

---

### Step 3: Find the XY Intersection (Causal Synthesis)

This is where understanding deepens. Ask:

1. **Which advantages were built intentionally 3-5+ years ago?** What long-term bets are now visible in today's competitive position?
2. **Which current weaknesses are legacy burdens?** What choices that were once rational now constrain behavior?
3. **When did historical momentum meet a competitive shift?** Did this phenomenon catch that wave or miss it?
4. **Where might X-axis momentum and Y-axis threats collide in the future?** What scenarios emerge?

**Output:** A causal narrative explaining why the current competitive position exists, which advantages are durable, and which are vulnerable.

**The key insight:** A competitor might have copied features (Y-axis), but they can't copy a 10-year head start in accumulated data and learning (X-axis). The gap exists because of when decisions were made, not just what decisions were made.

---

### Step 4: Validate with Feynman Verification

Can you explain this simply? Try these four checkpoints:

**Checkpoint 1: The 60-Second Explanation**
Can you tell the complete story in one paragraph, using language your grandmother would understand? If you need jargon or hedging ("probably," "seems like"), you're not there yet.

**Checkpoint 2: The Counterfactual**
"If [historical event] had gone differently, the outcome would have been [different], because..."
Can you complete that sentence? If not, you've missed a causal link.

**Checkpoint 3: The Prediction**
"If [future condition] occurs, [this thing] will [outcome], because..."
If you can't predict consequences of changes, the framework isn't integrated yet.

**Checkpoint 4: Gap Identification**
"I don't fully understand [specific aspect], which matters because..."
This signals genuine understanding—you know what you don't know.

**If you pass all four, you've reached 80% understanding.**

## Three Levels of Analysis

### Beginner: Quick Surface-Level Understanding (5-15 minutes)
**Web searches:** 5-7 queries
- Search `[name] history timeline` → Skim the X-axis history
- Search `[name] competitors [current year]` → Scan the Y-axis position
- Search `[name] why popular` → Identify one obvious XY intersection point

### Intermediate: Structured Understanding (30-60 minutes)
**Web searches:** 15-25 queries
- Complete all X-axis questions with web research (founding, decisions, inflection points)
- Complete all Y-axis questions with current competitive analysis
- Search recent news, reviews, metrics for accuracy
- Map 3-5 intersection insights: Historical decisions creating current position
- Attempt Feynman verification; identify gaps

### Advanced: Deep Causal Understanding (2-4 hours)
**Web searches:** 30-50 queries
- Build detailed X-axis timeline with specific dates and rationales
- Search for founder interviews, product announcements, press releases
- Quantify Y-axis positioning with current metrics and customer data
- Search industry analyst reports, market research, competitive analysis
- Trace how each major historical decision (X) shaped current competitive status (Y)
- Search for emerging threats, regulatory changes, industry trends
- Model future scenarios by extending X and Y axes
- Refine Feynman explanation through iteration
- Document sources with confidence ratings

## Common Patterns to Watch For

**Pattern 1: Historical Advantage Compounding**
An early technical decision (X-axis) becomes an insurmountable moat (Y-axis) because of accumulated learning. Netflix chose to produce original content in 2013; by 2023, they had 10 years of data competitors can't replicate.

**Pattern 2: Legacy Burden**
A choice that was optimal then (X-axis) becomes a constraint now (Y-axis). Nokia's Symbian OS was a smart choice in 2007; by 2012 it was an anchor because it couldn't be abandoned without restarting from zero.

**Pattern 3: Missed Wave**
The company didn't pivot when industry dynamics shifted. Instagram built for beautiful photos (Y-axis strength); when the world shifted to algorithmic discovery (X-axis shift), Instagram had to retrofit rather than architect from scratch like TikTok.

**Pattern 4: Right Place, Right Time**
A startup launched exactly when regulatory, technological, and cultural conditions aligned. TikTok chose algorithmic discovery in 2017-2018 (X-axis) just as the tech matured and social-graph platforms were losing favor (Y-axis).
